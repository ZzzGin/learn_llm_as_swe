# At What Stage of SFT Training Should Reinforcement Learning (RL) Be Introduced?

## What is SFT (Supervised Fine-Tuning)?

**Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)** is the process of adapting a raw, pre-trained base model (which is essentially a text-completion engine) to act as an assistant that understands and follows user instructions.

### How SFT is Done:

1. **Data Collection**: Collect a high-quality dataset of $(Prompt, Response)$ pairs. These pairs are typically authored by human annotators or generated by stronger teacher models (knowledge distillation).

2. **Training Objective**: The model is trained using **teacher forcing** with **Cross-Entropy Loss**. During training, the prompt is fed into the model, and the model is optimized to predict the next token of the target response.

3. **Loss Masking**: The training loss is calculated only on the tokens belonging to the *response*, ignoring the prompt. This ensures the model learns how to answer questions rather than how to write prompts.

---

## What is RL (Reinforcement Learning)?

**Reinforcement Learning (RL)**—often implemented as Reinforcement Learning from Human or AI Feedback (RLHF/RLAIF)—is the process of aligning a model's outputs with human values, safety guidelines, preferences, or objective correctness (such as math or coding execution). Instead of copying a single "correct" answer, the model explores different ways of responding and learns from a feedback signal (reward or penalty).

### How RL is Done:

RL is typically executed through one of three main paradigms:

#### 1. Actor-Critic / PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization)

* **Step 1: Train a Reward Model (RM)**: Collect a dataset of prompts with multiple candidate responses ranked by humans (e.g., Response A is better than Response B). Train a separate model (the RM) to output a scalar score representing the quality of a response.

* **Step 2: Policy Optimization**: The SFT model (the "Policy" or "Actor") generates responses to new prompts. The Reward Model scores these responses. An RL algorithm (like PPO) updates the policy's weights to maximize this reward while keeping a KL-divergence penalty to ensure the model doesn't drift too far from the original SFT model.

#### 2. Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)

* Rather than training a separate reward model and running complex actor-critic loops, **DPO** mathematically reformulates the RL objective. It optimizes the policy model directly on pairwise preference data ($Prompt, Winner\ Response, Loser\ Response$) by increasing the likelihood of the preferred response and decreasing the likelihood of the dispreferred response in a single step.

#### 3. Rule-Based / Outcome-Based RL (e.g., for Math and Coding)

* When objective truth is verifiable (like compiler feedback or mathematical proofs), the reward is calculated by hardcoded rules or test suites. The model is rewarded when its reasoning steps lead to the correct final answer (similar to how DeepSeek-R1 leverages RL to optimize reasoning paths).

---

## SFT vs. RL: Key Differences

| Feature | Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) | Reinforcement Learning (RL) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Learning Paradigm** | **Imitation Learning**: "Do exactly as the teacher does." | **Exploration & Optimization**: "Try different things, and do more of what works." |
| **Data Requirement** | High-quality, gold-standard $(Prompt, Response)$ pairs. | Preference pairs (Better vs. Worse) or automated reward functions/environments. |
| **Optimization Target** | Token-level: Maximizes the likelihood of predicting the exact ground-truth tokens. | Sequence-level: Maximizes the cumulative reward or preference margin of the entire output. |
| **Model Behavior** | Excellent for teaching syntax, formatting, and injecting specific domain knowledge. | Excellent for steering tone, reasoning, safety, self-correction, and general alignment. |
| **Limitation** | Capped by the quality of the training dataset; the model cannot easily surpass the "teacher". | Requires a stable reward signal; highly sensitive to hyperparameter tuning and reward hacking. |

## Why Must SFT Be Done First, and Not RL Directly?

1. **Limitations of Pre-trained Models**: Pre-trained models (such as GPT-3) are optimized for "next-token prediction" rather than "conversational instruction following." If you prompt them directly, they tend to continue appending internet text (for example, if you input "The capital of China is," it might write "The capital of the US is..."), instead of actually answering the question.

2. **The RL Exploration Space is Too Large, Lacking Positive Feedback (The Cold Start Problem)**:

   * Reinforcement learning requires the agent to receive sufficient positive feedback (reward) from the environment to converge properly.

   * If you perform RL (such as PPO) directly on a base model without SFT, taking a math problem as an example, sampling 100 times might not yield even a single standard Q&A format (as it keeps doing meaningless text completion). This leads to extremely low rewards, making it impossible for the model to learn.

3. **The Role of SFT**: Serving as a cold-start phase (similar to how DeepSeek-R1 uses a small amount of high-quality data for a cold start, rather than the pure RL of DeepSeek-R1-Zero), SFT quickly teaches the model basic conversational formats and instruction following, providing a reasonable starting point for RL.

## At What Point in SFT Training Should You Transition to RL? (Three Evaluation Dimensions)

### 1. Has SFT Been Sufficiently Trained?

* **Format and Instruction Alignment**: The model can stably follow instructions (e.g., reliably outputting in JSON format, recognizing specific language instructions, and not answering off-topic) without frequent repetition or formatted chaos.

* **Performance Reaching a Ceiling (Bottleneck)**: SFT loss drops to an extremely low level and stops fluctuating, with no significant improvement in model performance. At this stage, SFT is essentially pushing the limits of mimicking the training data and can hardly surpass the ceiling of the dataset itself, necessitating the introduction of RL.

### 2. Is There Still Ample Room for Improvement?

* **Pass@k is Much Larger than Pass@1**: If the probability of getting at least one correct answer out of $K$ samples (Pass@k) is significantly higher than the accuracy of sampling just once (Pass@1), it indicates that the model "has the ability" to answer correctly but lacks stability. In this case, RL can be used to guide the model to favor high-probability correct answers in a single generation.

* **Relatively Even Reward Distribution**: When sampling multiple answers for the same prompt, the resulting reward distribution contains both high and low scores (a relatively balanced distribution). This indicates that the model already knows how to answer, but the quality is inconsistent—precisely the ideal moment for RL to step in and optimize the generation probability distribution. *(If the sampled rewards are all extremely low, it means SFT is not yet fully trained, and SFT should be continued).*

### 3. Annotation Costs and Commercial Efficiency

* **High Cost of SFT**: SFT requires humans to write/annotate complete, high-quality Prompt-Response pairs, which is extremely expensive.

* **Low Cost and High Efficiency of RL**: In the RL phase, the model can generate candidate answers on its own, and humans or AI (RLAIF) only need to rank these candidates by preference, significantly reducing annotation costs.

## In Which Scenarios is RL More Suitable or Necessary?

1. **Scenarios Requiring Negative Feedback Correction**:

   * For example, safety alignment (refusing harmful content such as violence, drugs, pornography, and politically sensitive topics).

   * SFT is good at teaching the model "what it should say" (fitting), but struggles to teach "what it should not say." On the other hand, RL with penalty terms (such as PPO, DPO) can highly effectively suppress the model's harmful tendencies.

2. **Scenarios with No Single Standard Answer**:

   * For example, subjective and open-ended tasks like writing creative stories or providing consulting advice.

   * SFT can only rigidly mimic single samples, making it difficult to foster creativity. Conversely, RL can rank multiple generated outcomes by preference (for instance, comparing which one is more humorous or professional) to guide the model toward producing more diverse, creative, and high-quality responses.

---

## Core Conclusion

* **SFT Excels at Memorization**: It helps the model fit and remember the knowledge and formatting present in the training data.

* **RL Excels at Generalization**: Through policy sampling and comparison, it helps the model achieve better generalization and reasoning capabilities on unseen problems.
